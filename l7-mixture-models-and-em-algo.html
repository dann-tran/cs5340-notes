Mixture Models and the EM Algorithm

Latent variable models (LVMs)

An LVM p(x,z∣θ)p(\mathbf x, \mathbf z|\bm\theta)p(x,z∣θ) is a probability distribution over two sets of variables x,z\mathbf x, \mathbf zx,z where:

  • variables xn∈Rd\mathbf x_n\in\mathbb{R}^dxn​∈Rd are observed at learning time in a dataset D={x1,...,xN}\mathcal{D}=\{\mathbf x_1, ..., \mathbf x_N\}D={x1​,...,xN​}, and
  • latent/hidden variables zn\mathbf z_nzn​ are never observed.

Example: Gaussian mixture models (GMMs)

Our directed model: p(x,z∣θ)=p(x∣z,θ)p(z∣θ)p(\mathbf x, z|\bm\theta)=p(\mathbf x|z, \bm\theta)p(z|\bm\theta)p(x,z∣θ)=p(x∣z,θ)p(z∣θ). Here zzz is a scalar.

  • p(z∣θ)=Cat(z∣π)p(z|\bm\theta)=\text{Cat}(z|\bm\pi)p(z∣θ)=Cat(z∣π). Specifically, π\bm\piπ is a probability vector where p(z=k)=πkp(z=k)=\pi_kp(z=k)=πk​.
  • p(x∣z=k,θ)=N(x∣μk,Σk)p(\mathbf x|z=k, \bm\theta)=\mathcal{N}(\mathbf x|\bm\mu_k, \bm\Sigma_k)p(x∣z=k,θ)=N(x∣μk​,Σk​).

This model postulates that our observed data is comprised of KKK clusters with proportions specified by π1,...πK\pi_1, ...\pi_Kπ1​,...πK​; the distribution within each cluster is a Gaussian. We can see that p(x)p(\mathbf x)p(x) is a mixture by explicitly writing

p(x)=∑k=1Kp(x∣z=k)p(z=k)=∑k=1KπkN(x∣μk,Σk)p(\mathbf x)=\sum_{k=1}^Kp(\mathbf x|z=k)p(z=k)=\sum_{k=1}^K\pi_k\mathcal{N}(\mathbf x|\bm\mu_k, \bm\Sigma_k)p(x)=k=1∑K​p(x∣z=k)p(z=k)=k=1∑K​πk​N(x∣μk​,Σk​)

To generate a new data point, we sample a cluster kkk and then sample its Gaussian N(x∣μk,Σk)\mathcal{N}(\mathbf x|\bm\mu_k, \bm\Sigma_k)N(x∣μk​,Σk​).

Reasons for modelling latent variables

  • Some data might be naturally unobserved. E.g. patients dropping out during a clinical trials.

  • LVMs enable us to leverage our prior knowledge when defining a model. E.g. topic modelling of news articles as a mixture of KKK distinct distributions (one for each topic).

  • LVMs increase the expressive power of our model.

Marginal likelihood training

Our goal is still to fit the marginal likelihood p(X)p(\mathbf X)p(X). Applying the argument for KL divergence, we should maximize the observed data log-likelihood

ℓ(θ)=log⁡p(D∣θ)=∑x∈Dlog⁡p(x∣θ)=∑x∈Dlog⁡(∑zp(x∣z,θ)p(z))\ell(\bm\theta)=\log p(\mathcal D|\bm\theta)=\sum_{\mathbf x\in \mathcal D}\log p(\mathbf x|\bm\theta)=\sum_{\mathbf x\in \mathcal D}\log\left(\sum_{\mathbf z} p(\mathbf x|\mathbf z, \bm\theta)p(\mathbf z)\right)ℓ(θ)=logp(D∣θ)=x∈D∑​logp(x∣θ)=x∈D∑​log(z∑​p(x∣z,θ)p(z))

This optimization objective is considerably more difficult than regular log-likelihood, even for directed graphical models. Reasons:

  • The summation inside the log makes it impossible to decompose p(x)p(\mathbf x)p(x) into a sum of log-factors. Even if the model is directed, we can no longer derive a simple closed form expression for the parameters.
  • Whereas a single exponential family distribution p(x)p(\mathbf x)p(x) has a concave log-likelihood, the log of a weighted mixture of such distributions is no longer concave or convex (contrast with complete data log-likelihood ℓc(θ)\ell_c(\bm\theta)ℓc​(θ) which is concave). This non-convexity requires the development of specialized learning algorithms.

Learning LVMs

The Expectation-Maximization (EM) algo

Consider the complete data log-likelihood

ℓc(θ)≜log⁡p(X,Z∣θ)=∑i=1Nlog⁡p(xn,zn∣θ)\ell_c(\bm\theta) \triangleq \log p(\mathbf X, \mathbf Z|\bm\theta) = \sum_{i=1}^N\log p(\mathbf x_n, \mathbf z_n|\bm\theta)ℓc​(θ)≜logp(X,Z∣θ)=i=1∑N​logp(xn​,zn​∣θ)

If latent Z\mathbf ZZ were fully observed, we could compute ℓc(θ)\ell_c(\bm\theta)ℓc​(θ) and optimize it. However, since Z\mathbf ZZ is unknown:

  • we'll instead rely on a "soft" assignment of Z\mathbf ZZ in the form of a posterior p(Z∣X,θ)p(\textbf Z|\textbf X, \bm\theta)p(Z∣X,θ) to optimize the log-likelihood w.r.t. the parameters;
  • the posterior p(Z∣X,θ)p(\textbf Z|\textbf X, \bm\theta)p(Z∣X,θ) can often be efficiently computed if the parameters are known (an assumption, not ture for some models).

Our resulting tractable objective, auxillary function Q\mathcal QQ, is the expected complete data log-likelihood under latent variable distribution:

Q(θ,θold)=EZ∼p(Z∣X,θold)[ℓc(θ)]\mathcal{Q}(\bm\theta, \bm\theta^{\text{old}})=\mathbb{E}_{\textbf Z\sim p(\textbf Z|\textbf X, \bm\theta^{\text{old}})}[\ell_c(\bm\theta)]Q(θ,θold)=EZ∼p(Z∣X,θold)​[ℓc​(θ)]

EM follows a simple iterative two-step strategy:

  • Given an estimate θold\bm\theta^{\text{old}}θold of the parameters, compute posterior p(Z∣X,θold)p(\textbf Z|\textbf X, \bm\theta^{\text{old}})p(Z∣X,θold) as the expected sufficient statistics for our MLE. This can be seen as a "soft" assignment of xn\mathbf x_nxn​ to KKK clusters (a hard assignment would assign xnx_nxn​ to a single cluster).
  • Then, find a new estimate θnew\bm\theta^{\text{new}}θnew by optimizing Q\mathcal{Q}Q w.r.t. θ\bm\thetaθ. This process will eventually converge.

EM algo

  1. Start at an initial θold\theta^{\text{old}}θold.

  2. E-step: Compute the posterior p(Z∣X,θold)p(\textbf Z|\textbf X, \bm\theta^{\text{old}})p(Z∣X,θold)

  3. M-step: Compute the new paramters

θnew=arg max⁡θQ(θ,θold)\bm\theta^{\text{new}}=\argmax_\theta\mathcal{Q}(\bm\theta, \bm\theta^{\text{old}})θnew=θargmax​Q(θ,θold)
  1. Check for convergence of either the log-likelihood or the parameter values. If not converged, θold←θnew\bm\theta^{\text{old}}\leftarrow\bm\theta^{\text{new}}θold←θnew and return to step 2.

To perform MAP estimation, we modify the M-step as follows:

θnew=arg max⁡θQ(θ,θold)+log⁡p(θ)\bm\theta^{\text{new}}=\argmax_{\bm\theta}\mathcal{Q}(\bm\theta, \bm\theta^{\text{old}}) + \log p(\bm\theta)θnew=θargmax​Q(θ,θold)+logp(θ)

μk=∑nrnkxirkΣk=∑nrnk(xn−μk)(xn−μk)Trk=∑nrnkxnxnTrk−μkμkT\begin{align*} \bm\mu_k&=\frac{\sum_n r_{nk}\mathbf x_i}{r_k} \\ \bm\Sigma_k&=\frac{\sum_n r_{nk}(\mathbf x_n-\bm\mu_k)(\mathbf x_n - \bm\mu_k)^T}{r_k} = \frac{\sum_n r_{nk}\mathbf x_n\mathbf x_n^T}{r_k} - \bm\mu_k\bm\mu_k^T \end{align*}μk​Σk​​=rk​∑n​rnk​xi​​=rk​∑n​rnk​(xn​−μk​)(xn​−μk​)T​=rk​∑n​rnk​xn​xnT​​−μk​μkT​​

Properties of EM

Convergence is guaranteed for EM because:

  • The marginal likelihood increases after each EM iteration.
  • The marginal likelihood is upper-bounded by its true global maximum

However, since the objective is non-convex, we have no gurantee to find the global optimum. In fact, EM in practice converges almost always to a local optimum, and moreover, that optimum heavily depends on the choice of initialization. Thus it is very common to use multiple restarts of the algorithm and choose the best one in the end.
