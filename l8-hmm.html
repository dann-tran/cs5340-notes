Hidden Markov Models (HMMs)

The graphical model

Sequential data: datasets in which successive samples are no longer assumed to be independent.

Markov models

For discrete time steps:

p(x)=p(x1)∏t=2Tp(xt∣xt−1)p(x)=p(x_1)\prod_{t=2}^Tp(x_t|x_{t-1})p(x)=p(x1​)t=2∏T​p(xt​∣xt−1​)

If the transition probability p(xt∣xt−1)p(x_t|x_{t-1})p(xt​∣xt−1​) is independent of time, then the chain is called homogenous, stationary, or time-invariant.

If xtx_txt​ is discrete, we can represent p(xt∣xt−1)p(x_t|x_{t-1})p(xt​∣xt−1​) as a transition matrix AAA, where Aij=p(Xt=j∣Xt−1=i)A_{ij}=p(X_t=j|X_{t-1}=i)Aij​=p(Xt​=j∣Xt−1​=i). Each row of the matrix sums to one, ∑jAij=1\sum_j A_{ij}=1∑j​Aij​=1, so this is called a stochastic matrix.

HMM

An HMM is a natural generalization of a mixture model, viewed as a "dynamical" mixture model, where we no longer assume that the states (i.e. mixture components) are chosen independently at each step, but that the choice of a state at a given step depends on the choice of the state at the previous step. Thus we augment the basic mixture model to include a matrix of transition probabilites linking the states at neighbouring steps.

A HMM consists of a discrete-time, discrete-state Markov chain, plus an observation model aka emission probability p(xt∣zt)p(\mathbf x_t|z_t)p(xt​∣zt​). The joint distribution:

p(z1:T,x1:T∣θ)=p(z1:T∣θ)p(x1:T∣z,θ)=p(z1∣π)∏t=2Tp(zt∣zt−1,A)∏t=1Tp(xt∣zt,ϕ)p(\mathbf z_{1:T}, \mathbf x_{1:T}|\bm\theta) =p(\mathbf z_{1:T}|\bm\theta)p(\mathbf x_{1:T}|z, \bm\theta) =p(z_1|\bm\pi)\prod_{t=2}^Tp(z_t|z_{t-1}, A)\prod_{t=1}^Tp(\mathbf x_t|z_t, \bm\phi)p(z1:T​,x1:T​∣θ)=p(z1:T​∣θ)p(x1:T​∣z,θ)=p(z1​∣π)t=2∏T​p(zt​∣zt−1​,A)t=1∏T​p(xt​∣zt​,ϕ)

where

  • Parameters θ={π,A,ϕ}\bm\theta=\{\bm\pi, A, \bm\phi\}θ={π,A,ϕ}
  • Initial state distribution p(z1=i)=πip(z_1=i)=\pi_ip(z1​=i)=πi​
  • Transition matrix Ai,j=p(zt=j∣zt−1=i)A_{i, j}=p(z_t=j|z_{t-1}=i)Ai,j​=p(zt​=j∣zt−1​=i)
  • Class-conditional or emission densities p(xt∣zt=k,ϕ)p(\mathbf x_t|z_t=k, \bm\phi)p(xt​∣zt​=k,ϕ). E.g.
    • Discrete: p(xt=l∣zt=k,ϕ)=Bk,lp(\mathbf x_t=l|z_t=k, \bm\phi)=B_{k, l}p(xt​=l∣zt​=k,ϕ)=Bk,l​ where BBBis an observation matrix.
    • Continuous: p(xt∣zt=k,ϕ)=N(xt∣μk,Σk)p(\mathbf x_t|z_t=k, \bm\phi)=\mathcal N(\mathbf x_t|\bm\mu_k, \bm\Sigma_k)p(xt​∣zt​=k,ϕ)=N(xt​∣μk​,Σk​)

Inference in HMMs

Types of inference problems for temporal models

Filtering: computes the belief state p(zt∣x1:t)p(z_t|\mathbf x_{1:t})p(zt​∣x1:t​) online as the data streams in.

Smoothing: computes p(zt∣x1:t)p(z_t|\mathbf x_{1:t})p(zt​∣x1:t​) offline, given all the evidence.

Fixed-lag smoothing: computes p(zt−ℓ∣x1:t)p(z_{t-\ell}|\mathbf x_{1:t})p(zt−ℓ​∣x1:t​) where ℓ>0\ell>0ℓ>0 is the lag. This is a compromise between online and offline estimation and gives better performance than filtering, but incurs a slight delay. By changing the size of the lag, one can trade off accuracy vs delay.

Prediction: computes p(zt+h∣x1:t)p(z_{t+h}|\mathbf x_{1:t})p(zt+h​∣x1:t​) where h>0h>0h>0 is the prediction horizon.

MAP estimation: computes arg max⁡z1:Tp(z1:T∣x1:T)\argmax_{\mathbf z_{1:T}}p(\mathbf z_{1:T}|\mathbf x_{1:T})argmaxz1:T​​p(z1:T​∣x1:T​). This is known as Viterbi decoding in the context of HMMs.

Posterior samples: z1:T∼p(z1:T∣x1:T)\mathbf z_{1:T}\sim p(\mathbf z_{1:T}|\mathbf x_{1:T})z1:T​∼p(z1:T​∣x1:T​). This can be done when there is more than one plausiable interpretation of the data.

Probability of the eivdence: p(x1:T)=∑z1:Tp(z1:T,x1:T)p(\mathbf x_{1:T})=\sum_{\mathbf z_{1:T}}p(\mathbf z_{1:T}, \mathbf x_{1:T})p(x1:T​)=∑z1:T​​p(z1:T​,x1:T​)

The forwards algo (online)

Goal: compute filtered marginal αt=p(zt∣x1:t)\bm\alpha_t=p(z_t|\mathbf x_{1:t})αt​=p(zt​∣x1:t​) aka filtered belief state at time ttt.

Prediction step: computes the one-step-ahead predictive density; this acts as the new prior for time ttt.

p(zt=j∣x1:t−1)=∑ip(zt=j∣zt−1=i)p(zt−1=i∣x1:t−1)p(z_t=j|\mathbf x_{1:t-1})=\sum_i p(z_t=j|z_{t-1}=i)p(z_{t-1}=i|\mathbf x_{1:t-1})p(zt​=j∣x1:t−1​)=i∑​p(zt​=j∣zt−1​=i)p(zt−1​=i∣x1:t−1​)

Update step: absorved the observed data from time ttt using Bayes rule

αt(j)≜p(zt=j∣x1:t)=1Ztp(xt∣zt=j)p(zt=j∣x1:t−1)=1Ztψt(j)∑iαt−1(j)ψ(i,j)\begin{align*} \alpha_t(j) &\triangleq p(z_t=j|\mathbf x_{1:t}) \\ &=\frac 1{Z_t}p(\mathbf x_t|z_t=j)p(z_t=j|\mathbf x_{1:t-1}) \\ &=\frac 1{Z_t}\psi_t(j)\sum_i\alpha_{t-1}(j)\psi(i, j) \end{align*}αt​(j)​≜p(zt​=j∣x1:t​)=Zt​1​p(xt​∣zt​=j)p(zt​=j∣x1:t−1​)=Zt​1​ψt​(j)i∑​αt−1​(j)ψ(i,j)​

where Zt≜p(xt∣x1:t−1)=∑jψt(j)∑iαt−1(j)ψ(i,j)Z_t\triangleq p(\mathbf x_t|\mathbf x_{1:t-1})=\sum_j \psi_t(j)\sum_i\alpha_{t-1}(j)\psi(i, j)Zt​≜p(xt​∣x1:t−1​)=∑j​ψt​(j)∑i​αt−1​(j)ψ(i,j)

In matrix vector notation:

αt∝ψt⊙(ΨTαt−1)\bm\alpha_t\propto\bm\psi_t\odot(\bm\Psi^T\bm\alpha_{t-1})αt​∝ψt​⊙(ΨTαt−1​)

where ψt(j)=p(xt∣zt=j)\psi_t(j)=p(\mathbf x_t|z_t=j)ψt​(j)=p(xt​∣zt​=j) is the local evidence at time ttt, Ψ(i,j)=p(pt=j∣zt−1=i)\Psi(i, j)=p(p_t=j|z_{t-1}=i)Ψ(i,j)=p(pt​=j∣zt−1​=i) is the transition matrix.

Base case:

α1(j)=p(z1=j∣x1)=ψ1(j)\alpha_1(j)=p(z_1=j|\mathbf x_1)=\psi_1(j)α1​(j)=p(z1​=j∣x1​)=ψ1​(j)

The forward-backward algo (offline)

Goal: compute the smoothed posterior marginal γt(j)≜p(zt=j∣x1:T)\gamma_t(j)\triangleq p(z_t=j|\mathbf x_{1:T})γt​(j)≜p(zt​=j∣x1:T​).

γt(j)=p(zt=j∣x1:T)∝p(zt=j,xt+1:T∣x1:t)∝p(zt=j∣x1:t)p(xt+1:T∣zt=j)=αt(j)βt(j)\begin{align*} \gamma_t(j) &=p(z_t=j|\mathbf x_{1:T}) \\ &\propto p(z_t=j, \mathbf x_{t+1:T}|\mathbf x_{1:t}) \\ &\propto p(z_t=j|\mathbf x_{1:t})p(\mathbf x_{t+1:T}|z_t=j) \\ &=\alpha_t(j)\beta_t(j) \end{align*}γt​(j)​=p(zt​=j∣x1:T​)∝p(zt​=j,xt+1:T​∣x1:t​)∝p(zt​=j∣x1:t​)p(xt+1:T​∣zt​=j)=αt​(j)βt​(j)​

where βj(j)≜p(xt+1:T∣zt=j)\beta_j(j)\triangleq p(\mathbf x_{t+1:T}|z_t=j)βj​(j)≜p(xt+1:T​∣zt​=j) is the conditional likelhiood of future evidence given the hidden state at time ttt is jjj.

The forwards algo recursively computes the α\alphaα's in a left-to-right fashion. We'll recursively compute the β\betaβ's in a right-to-left fashion.

βt−1(i)=p(xt:T∣zt−1=i)=∑jp(zt=j,xt:T∣zt−1=i)=∑jp(xt+1:T∣zt=j)p(xt∣zt=j)p(zt=j∣zt−1=i)=∑jβt(j)ψt(j)ψ(i,j)\begin{align*} \beta_{t-1}(i) &=p(\mathbf x_{t:T}|z_{t-1}=i) \\ &=\sum_j p(z_t=j, \mathbf x_{t:T}|z_{t-1}=i) \\ &=\sum_j p(\mathbf x_{t+1:T}|z_t=j)p(\mathbf x_t|z_t=j)p(z_t=j|z_{t-1}=i) \\ &=\sum_j \beta_t(j)\psi_t(j)\psi(i, j) \end{align*}βt−1​(i)​=p(xt:T​∣zt−1​=i)=j∑​p(zt​=j,xt:T​∣zt−1​=i)=j∑​p(xt+1:T​∣zt​=j)p(xt​∣zt​=j)p(zt​=j∣zt−1​=i)=j∑​βt​(j)ψt​(j)ψ(i,j)​

βt−1=Ψ(ψt⊙βt)\bm\beta_{t-1}=\bm\Psi(\bm\psi_t\odot\bm\beta_t)βt−1​=Ψ(ψt​⊙βt​)

Two-slice smoothed marginals

When we use EM for learning, we'll need to compute the expected number of transitions from state iii to state jjj:

Nij=∑t=1T−1E[I(zt=i,zt+1=j)∣x1:T]=∑t=1Tp(zt=i,zt+1=j∣x1:T)\begin{align*} N_{ij} &=\sum_{t=1}^{T-1}\mathbb E[\mathbb I(z_t=i, z_{t+1}=j)|\mathbb x_{1:T}] \\ &=\sum_{t=1}^Tp(z_t=i, z_{t+1}=j|\mathbb x_{1:T}) \end{align*}Nij​​=t=1∑T−1​E[I(zt​=i,zt+1​=j)∣x1:T​]=t=1∑T​p(zt​=i,zt+1​=j∣x1:T​)​

ξt,t+1(i,j)≜p(zt=i,zt+1=j∣x1:T)∝p(zt∣x1:t)p(zt+1∣zt,xt+1:T)∝p(zt∣x1:t)p(xt+1:T∣zt,zt+1)p(zt+1∣zt)∝p(zt∣x1:t)p(xt+1∣zt+1)p(xt+2:T∣zt+1)p(zt+1∣zt)=αt(i)ψt+1(j)βt+1(j)ψ(i,j)\begin{align*} \xi_{t, t+1}(i, j) &\triangleq p(z_t=i, z_{t+1}=j|\mathbb x_{1:T}) \\ &\propto p(z_t|\mathbf x_{1:t})p(z_{t+1}|z_t, \mathbf x_{t+1:T}) \\ &\propto p(z_t|\mathbf x_{1:t})p(\mathbf x_{t+1:T}|z_t, z_{t+1})p(z_{t+1}|z_t) \\ &\propto p(z_t|\mathbf x_{1:t})p(\mathbf x_{t+1}|z_{t+1})p(\mathbf x_{t+2:T}|z_{t+1})p(z_{t+1}|z_t) \\ &=\alpha_t(i)\psi_{t+1}(j)\beta_{t+1}(j)\psi(i, j) \end{align*}ξt,t+1​(i,j)​≜p(zt​=i,zt+1​=j∣x1:T​)∝p(zt​∣x1:t​)p(zt+1​∣zt​,xt+1:T​)∝p(zt​∣x1:t​)p(xt+1:T​∣zt​,zt+1​)p(zt+1​∣zt​)∝p(zt​∣x1:t​)p(xt+1​∣zt+1​)p(xt+2:T​∣zt+1​)p(zt+1​∣zt​)=αt​(i)ψt+1​(j)βt+1​(j)ψ(i,j)​

Time and space complexity

A straightforward implementation of FB takes O(K2T)O(K^2T)O(K2T) time since we must perform a K×KK\times KK×K matrix multiplication at each step. If the transition matrix is sparse, we can reduce this substantially e.g. O(TK)O(TK)O(TK) for a left-to-right transition matrix.

The expected sufficient statistics needed by EM are ∑tξt−1,t(i,j)\sum_t\xi_{t-1, t}(i, j)∑t​ξt−1,t​(i,j) which takes constant space. However, to compute them, we need O(KT)O(KT)O(KT) working space, since we must store {αt}t=1T\{\alpha_t\}_{t=1}^T{αt​}t=1T​ until we do the backwards pass. It is possible to devise a simple divide-and-conquer algo that reduces the space complexity from O(KT)O(KT)O(KT) to O(Klog⁡T)O(K\log T)O(KlogT) at the cost of increasing the running time from O(K2T)O(K^2T)O(K2T) to o(K2Tlog⁡T)o(K^2T\log T)o(K2TlogT).

The Viterbi algo

The Viterbi algo computes the most probable sequence of states in a chain-structured grpahical model i.e. MAP

z∗=arg max⁡z1:Tp(z1:t∣x1:T)\mathbf z^*=\argmax_{\mathbf z_{1:T}}p(\mathbf z_{1:t}|\mathbf x_{1:T})z∗=z1:T​argmax​p(z1:t​∣x1:T​)

This is equivalent to computing a shortest path through the trellis diagram where the nodes are possible states at each time step, and the node and edge weights are log-probabilities i.e. the weight of a path (zt)t=1T(z_t)_{t=1}^T(zt​)t=1T​ is given by

log⁡π1(z1)+log⁡ψ1(z1)+∑t=2T[log⁡ψ(zt−1,zt)+log⁡ψt(zt)]\log\pi_1(z_1)+\log\psi_1(z_1)+\sum_{t=2}^T[\log\psi(z_{t-1}, z_t)+\log\psi_t(z_t)]logπ1​(z1​)+logψ1​(z1​)+t=2∑T​[logψ(zt−1​,zt​)+logψt​(zt​)]

We cannot simply replace the sum-operator in forwards-backwards (sum-product) with a max-operator. In general max-product can lead to incorrect results if there are multiple equally probably joint assignments. This is because each node breaks ties independently and hence may do so in a manner that is inconssitent with its neighbours.

The Viterbi algo uses max-product for the forward pass and a traceback procedure for backward pass to recover the most probable path through the trellis of states. Once ztz_tzt​ pics its most probable state, the previous nodes condition on this event, and therefore they will break ties consistently.

Define the probability of ending up in state jjj at time ttt, given that we take the most probable path.

δt(j)≜max⁡z1,...,zt−1p(z1:t−1,zt=j∣x1:t)\delta_t(j)\triangleq\max_{z_1, ..., z_{t-1}} p(\mathbf z_{1:t-1}, z_t=j|\mathbf x_{1:t})δt​(j)≜z1​,...,zt−1​max​p(z1:t−1​,zt​=j∣x1:t​)

The most probable path to state jjj at time ttt must consist of the most probable path to some other state iii at time t−1t-1t−1, followed by a transition from iii to jjj. Hence

δt(j)=max⁡iδt−1(i)ψ(i,j)ψt(j)\delta_t(j)=\max_i \delta_{t-1}(i)\psi(i, j)\psi_t(j)δt​(j)=imax​δt−1​(i)ψ(i,j)ψt​(j)

We also keep track of the most likely previous state on the most probable path to zt=jz_t=jzt​=j for all jjj:

at(j)=arg max⁡iδt−1(i)ψ(i,j)ψt(j)a_t(j)=\argmax_i\delta_{t-1}(i)\psi(i, j)\psi_t(j)at​(j)=iargmax​δt−1​(i)ψ(i,j)ψt​(j)

We initialize by setting δ1(j)=πjψ1(j)\delta_1(j)=\pi_j\psi_1(j)δ1​(j)=πj​ψ1​(j) and terminate by computing the most probable final state zT∗z_T^*zT∗​:

zT∗=arg max⁡iδT(i)z_T^*=\argmax_i\delta_T(i)zT∗​=iargmax​δT​(i)

We can then compute the most probable sequence of states using traceback:

zt∗=at+1(zt+1∗)z_t^*=a_{t+1}(z_{t+1}^*)zt∗​=at+1​(zt+1∗​)

Numerical underflow: work in log domain. We can use

log⁡δt(j)≜max⁡z1:t−1log⁡p(z1:t−1,zt=j∣x1:t)=max⁡ilog⁡δt−1(i)+log⁡ψ(i,j)+log⁡ψt(j)\begin{align*} \log\delta_t(j) &\triangleq \max_{\mathbf z_{1:t-1}}\log p(\mathbf z_{1:t-1}, z_t=j|\mathbf x_{1:t}) \\ &=\max_i\log\delta_{t-1}(i)+\log\psi(i, j)+\log\psi_t(j) \end{align*}logδt​(j)​≜z1:t−1​max​logp(z1:t−1​,zt​=j∣x1:t​)=imax​logδt−1​(i)+logψ(i,j)+logψt​(j)​
