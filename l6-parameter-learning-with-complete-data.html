Learning methods

Maximum likelihood estimate (MLE)

θ^≜arg max⁡θp(D∣θ)=arg max⁡θlog⁡p(D∣θ)\begin{align*} \hat\theta &\triangleq \argmax_{\theta}p(\mathcal D|\theta) \\ &= \argmax_{\theta}\log p(\mathcal D|\theta) \end{align*}θ^​≜θargmax​p(D∣θ)=θargmax​logp(D∣θ)​

Maximum a posteriori (MAP) estiamte

θ^≜arg max⁡θp(θ∣D)=arg max⁡θp(D∣θ)p(θ)=arg max⁡θ[log⁡p(D∣θ)+log⁡p(θ)]\begin{align*} \hat\theta &\triangleq \argmax_{\theta}p(\theta|\mathcal D) \\ &= \argmax_{\theta}p(\mathcal D|\theta)p(\theta) \\ &= \argmax_{\theta}[\log p(\mathcal D|\theta)+\log p(\theta)] \end{align*}θ^​≜θargmax​p(θ∣D)=θargmax​p(D∣θ)p(θ)=θargmax​[logp(D∣θ)+logp(θ)]​

Note that under strong sampling assumption, p(D∣θ)=[1∣θ∣]Ip(\mathcal D|\theta)=\left[\frac 1 {\vert\theta\vert}\right]^Ip(D∣θ)=[∣θ∣1​]I. Since the likelihood p(D∣θ)p(\mathcal D|\theta)p(D∣θ) depends exponentially on N, and the prior p(θ)p(\theta)p(θ) stays constant, as we get more and more data, the MAP estimate converges towards the MLE. In other words, if we have enoughd ata, the data overwhelms the prior.

The Bayesian approach

Whereas MLE and MAP give point estimates of θ\thetaθ, the Bayesian approach uses the posterior p(θ∣D)=p(D∣θ)p(θ)p(D)p(\theta|\mathcal D)=\frac{p(\mathcal D|\theta)p(\theta)}{p(\mathcal D)}p(θ∣D)=p(D)p(D∣θ)p(θ)​ to evaluate the predictive distribution i.e. admit many values of the parameters compatible with the data and weigh them according to the posterior density.

p(x∣D)=∫p(x∣θ)p(θ∣D)dθp(\mathbf x|\mathcal D)=\int p(\mathbf x|\theta)p(\theta|\mathcal D)d\thetap(x∣D)=∫p(x∣θ)p(θ∣D)dθ

From the Bayesian approach, the posteriors for MAP and MLE can be considered as delta functions centered at their estimates θ^\hat\thetaθ^ i.e. p(θ∣D)=δ(θ−θ^)p(\theta|\mathcal D)=\delta(\theta-\hat\theta)p(θ∣D)=δ(θ−θ^). Then,

p(x∣D)=∫p(x∣θ)δ(θ−θ^)dθ=p(x∣θ^)p(\mathbf x|\mathcal D)=\int p(\mathbf x|\theta)\delta(\theta-\hat\theta)d\theta=p(\mathbf x|\hat\theta)p(x∣D)=∫p(x∣θ)δ(θ−θ^)dθ=p(x∣θ^)

Decomposable models

A graph is said to be decomposable if it can be recursively subdivided into disjoint sets AAA, BBB, and SSS where SSS separates AAA and BBB, and where SSS is complete.

We can find MLEs for decomposable grphs by inspection, but only if the potentials are defined on maximal cliques i.e. our parameterization must be s.t. the set C\mathcal CC ranges over the maximal cliques in the graph. Given this constraint, the recipe is the following:

  • for every clique CCC, set the clique potential to the empirical marginal for that clique,
  • for every non-empty intersection between cliques, associate an empirical marginal with that intersection, and divide that empirical marginal into the potential of one of the two cliques that form the intersection.

Example, for the figure below, we would have the following MLE

p^ML(x1,x2,x3,x4)=pemp(x1,x2,x3)pemp(x2,x3,x4)pemp(x2,x3)\hat p_{\text{ML}}(x_1, x_2, x_3, x_4)=\frac{p_\text{emp}(x_1, x_2, x_3)p_\text{emp}(x_2, x_3, x_4)}{p_\text{emp}(x_2, x_3)}p^​ML​(x1​,x2​,x3​,x4​)=pemp​(x2​,x3​)pemp​(x1​,x2​,x3​)pemp​(x2​,x3​,x4​)​

Iterative proportional fitting (IPF)

IPF converges and ascends an objective function at each step. It is both a fixed-point algorithm and a coordinate ascent algorithm.

IPF as fixed-point iteration

Let us return to the gradient of log-likelihood, but retain the ψC(xC)\psi_C(x_C)ψC​(xC​) factors. We have

pemp(xC)ψC(xC)=p(xC)ψC(xC)\frac{p_\text{emp}(x_C)}{\psi_C(x_C)}=\frac{p(x_C)}{\psi_C(x_C)}ψC​(xC​)pemp​(xC​)​=ψC​(xC​)p(xC​)​

Note that the parameter ψc(xC)\psi_c(x_C)ψc​(xC​) appears explicitly in this equation in two places, but also appears implicitly in the marginal p(xC)p(x_C)p(xC​). We can obtain an iterative algo by holding the values of ψC(xC)\psi_C(x_C)ψC​(xC​) fixed on the RHS, and solving for the free parameter ψC(xC)\psi_C(x_C)ψC​(xC​) on the LHS.

ψC(t+1)(xC)=ψC(t)(xC)pemp(xC)p(t)(xC)\psi_C^{(t+1)}(x_C)=\psi_C^{(t)}(x_C)\frac{p_\text{emp}(x_C)}{p^{(t)}(x_C)}ψC(t+1)​(xC​)=ψC(t)​(xC​)p(t)(xC​)pemp​(xC​)​

IPF for tabular MRFs

  1. Initialise ϕC=1\phi_C=1ϕC​=1 for C∈CC\in\mathcal CC∈C.
  2. Repeat until convergence, for C∈CC\in\mathcal CC∈C:
    • pC=p(xC∣ϕ)p_C=p(x_C|\phi)pC​=p(xC​∣ϕ).
    • p^C=pemp(x)\hat p_C=p_{\text{emp}}(x)p^​C​=pemp​(x).
    • ϕC=ϕC×p^CpC\phi_C=\phi_C\times\frac{\hat p_C}{p_C}ϕC​=ϕC​×pC​p^​C​​

Properties of the IPF update equation:

  • the marginal p(t+1)(xC)p^{(t+1)}(x_C)p(t+1)(xC​) is equal to the empirical marginal pemp(xC)p_\text{emp}(x_C)pemp​(xC​), and
  • the normalization factor ZZZ remains constant across IPF updates.

IPF as coordinate ascent

Gradient descent

ψC(t+1)(xC)=ψC(t)(xC)+ρψC(t)(xC)(pemp(xC)−p(t)(xC))\psi_C^{(t+1)}(x_C)=\psi_C^{(t)}(x_C)+\frac\rho{\psi_C^{(t)}(x_C)}\left(p_\text{emp}(x_C)-p^{(t)}(x_C)\right)ψC(t+1)​(xC​)=ψC(t)​(xC​)+ψC(t)​(xC​)ρ​(pemp​(xC​)−p(t)(xC​))

where ρ\rhoρ is a step size. We see that the difference between the empirical marginals and the model marginals drives the algorithm.

Advantage: All parameters can be adjusted silmultaneously (although a variant of IPF can also achieve this).

Disadvantages:

  • The need to choose a step size.
  • The noramlization factor ZZZ does not remain constant and must be recalculated anew after each iteration.
