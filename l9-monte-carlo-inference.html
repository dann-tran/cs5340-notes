Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)

MCMC is a strategy for generating samples x(i)x^{(i)}x(i) while exploring the state space X\mathcal{X}X using a Markov chain mechanism. This mechanism is consturcted so that the chain spends more time in the most important regions, i.e. samples x(i)x^{(i)}x(i) mimic samples drawn from target distribution p~(x)\tilde{p}(x)p~​(x).

Consider a discrete-time stochastic process x(i)x^{(i)}x(i) is called a Markov chain if it satisfies the Markov assumption:

Existence of a stationary distribution

The high-level idea of MCMC will be to construct a Markov chain whose states will be joint assignments to the variables in the model and whose stationary distribution will equal the model probability ppp.

Two sufficient conditions on TTT for finite-state Markov chain to have a stationary distribution:

  • Irreducibility: It is possible to get from any state xxx to any other state x′x'x′ with a positive probability in a finite number of steps. In other words, TTT cannot be reduced to separate smaller matrices annd the transition graph is connected.
  • Aperiodicity: The train should not be trapped in cycles. In other words, it is possible to return to any state at any time, i.e., there exists an nnn such that for all kkk and all n′≥nn'\geq nn′≥n, p(x(n′)=k∣x(n)=k)>0p(x^{(n')}=k|x^{(n)}=k)>0p(x(n′)=k∣x(n)=k)>0.

In the case of continuuous variables, the Markov chain must be ergodic.

A sufficient (but not necessary) condition that a particular distribution p(x)p(x)p(x) is a stationary distribution is the following detailed balance (reversibility) condition:

Proof: summing both sides over x(i−1)x^{(i-1)}x(i−1) gives us

MCMC samplers are irreducible and aperiodic Markov chains that have the target distribution as the invariant distribution. One way to design these samplers is to ensure that detailed balance is satisfied.

MCMC algorithms

At a high level, MCMC algorithms will have the following structure. They take as argument a transition operator TTT specifying a Markov chain whose stationary distribution is ppp (unnormalised), and an initial assignment x(0)x^{(0)}x(0) to the variables of ppp. An MCMC algorithm then perform the following steps.

  1. Run the Markov chain from x(0)x^{(0)}x(0) for BBB burn-in steps.
  2. Run the Markov chain for NNN sampling steps and collect all the states that it visits.

Assuming BBB is sufficiently large, the latter collection of states will form samples from ppp. We may then use these samples for Monte Carlo integration (or in importance sampling). We may also use them to:

  • produce Monte Carlo estimates of marginal probabilities,
  • perform MAP inference by take the sample with the highest probability

The Metropolis-Hastings (MH) algorithm is our first way to construct Markov chains within MCMC. The MH method constructs a transition operator TTT from two components:

T(x(i+1)∣x(i))=q(x(i+1)∣x(i))min⁡{1,p(x(i+1))q(x(i)∣x(i+1))p(x(i))q(x(i+1)∣x(i))}=1p(x(i))min⁡{p(x(i))q(x(i+1)∣x(i)),p(x(i+1))q(x(i)∣x(i+1))}  ⟹  T(x(i+1)∣x(i))p(x(i))=M(x(i+1),x(i))\begin{align*} T(x^{(i+1)}|x^{(i)}) &= q(x^{(i+1)}|x^{(i)})\min\left\{1, \frac{p(x^{(i+1)})q(x^{(i)}|x^{(i+1)})}{p(x^{(i)})q(x^{(i+1)}|x^{(i)})}\right\} \\ &= \frac{1}{p(x^{(i)})}\min\left\{p(x^{(i)})q(x^{(i+1)}|x^{(i)}), p(x^{(i+1)})q(x^{(i)}|x^{(i+1)})\right\} \\ \implies T(x^{(i+1)}|x^{(i)})p(x^{(i)}) &= M(x^{(i+1)},x^{(i)}) \end{align*}T(x(i+1)∣x(i))⟹T(x(i+1)∣x(i))p(x(i))​=q(x(i+1)∣x(i))min{1,p(x(i))q(x(i+1)∣x(i))p(x(i+1))q(x(i)∣x(i+1))​}=p(x(i))1​min{p(x(i))q(x(i+1)∣x(i)),p(x(i+1))q(x(i)∣x(i+1))}=M(x(i+1),x(i))​

where M(x(i+1),x(i))=min⁡{p(x(i))q(x(i+1)∣x(i)),p(x(i+1))q(x(i)∣x(i+1))}M(x^{(i+1)},x^{(i)})=\min\left\{p(x^{(i)})q(x^{(i+1)}|x^{(i)}), p(x^{(i+1)})q(x^{(i)}|x^{(i+1)})\right\}M(x(i+1),x(i))=min{p(x(i))q(x(i+1)∣x(i)),p(x(i+1))q(x(i)∣x(i+1))}. Likewhise, it can be shown that T(x(i)∣x(i+1))p(x(i+1))=M(x(i),x(i+1))T(x^{(i)}|x^{(i+1)})p(x^{(i+1)}) = M(x^{(i)},x^{(i+1)})T(x(i)∣x(i+1))p(x(i+1))=M(x(i),x(i+1)).

Observe that MMM is symmetric i.e. M(x(i+1),x(i))=M(x(i),x(i+1))M(x^{(i+1)},x^{(i)})=M(x^{(i)},x^{(i+1)})M(x(i+1),x(i))=M(x(i),x(i+1)). The detailed balance condition then follows.

To ensure that the MH algo converges:

  • Aperiodicity: no additional conditions since rejection is allowed.
  • Irreducibility: the support of qqq must include the support of ppp.

Fact 2: The normalising constant of the target distribution is not required.

Fact 3: Success of failure of the algo often hinges on the choice of qqq.

  • If the proposal is too narrow, only one mode of ppp might be visited.
  • If the proposal is too wide, the rejection rate can be very high, resulting in high correlations.
  • If all the modes are visited while the acceptance probability is high, the chain is said to "mix" well.

Below shows approximations obtained using the MH algorithm with three Gaussian proposal distributions of different variances.

Independent sampler

In the independent sampler, the proposal is independent of the current state, q(x∗∣x(i))=q(x∗)q(x^*|x^{(i)})=q(x^*)q(x∗∣x(i))=q(x∗). Hence the acceptance probability is

A(x∗,x(i))=min⁡{1,p(x∗)q(x(i))p(xi)q(x∗)}=min⁡{1,w(x∗)w(x(i))}\mathcal{A}(x^*, x^{(i)})=\min\left\{1, \frac{p(x^*)q(x^{(i)})}{p(x^{i})q(x^*)}\right\}=\min\left\{1, \frac{w(x^*)}{w(x^{(i)})}\right\}A(x∗,x(i))=min{1,p(xi)q(x∗)p(x∗)q(x(i))​}=min{1,w(x(i))w(x∗)​}

This algo is close to importance sampling, but now the samples are correlated since they result from comparing one sample to the other.

Metropolis algorithm

The Metropolis algo assumes a symmetric random walk proposal q(x∗∣x(i))=q(x(i)∣x∗)q(x^*|x^{(i)})=q(x^{(i)}|x^*)q(x∗∣x(i))=q(x(i)∣x∗) e.g. isotropic Gaussian. The acceptance ratio simplifies to

A(x∗,x(i))=min⁡{1,p(x∗)p(xi)}\mathcal{A}(x^*, x^{(i)})=\min\left\{1, \frac{p(x^*)}{p(x^{i})}\right\}A(x∗,x(i))=min{1,p(xi)p(x∗)​}
