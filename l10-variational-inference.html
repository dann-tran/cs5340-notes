Variational Inference

Inference as Optimization

The main idea of variational methods is to cast inference as an optimization problem.

Suppose we are given an intractable probability distribution ppp. Variational techniques will try to solve an optimization problem over a class of tractable distributions Q\mathcal{Q}Q in order to find a q∈Qq\in\mathcal{Q}q∈Q that is most similar to ppp. We will then query qqq (rather than ppp) in order to get an approximate solution.

Main differences between sampling and variational techniques:

  • Unlike sampling-based methods, variational approaches will almost never find the globally optimal solution.
  • However, we will always know if they have converged. In some cases, we will even have bounds on their accuracy.
  • In practice, variational inference methods often scale better and are more amenable to techniques like stochastic gradient optimization, parallelization over multiple processors, and acceleration using GPUs.

Setup:

  • Observed data D\mathcal DD.
  • Latent, unobserved variables x\mathbf xx.
  • Target distribution is the posterior p(x)=p(x∣D)p(\mathbf x)=p(\mathbf x|\mathcal D)p(x)=p(x∣D).
  • Unnormalized target distribution is the joint density p~(x)=p(x,D)\tilde p(\mathbf x)=p(\mathbf x, \mathcal D)p~​(x)=p(x,D).
  • Normalisation constant is the likelihood Z=p(D)=∫Zp(x,D)Z=p(\mathcal D)=\int_{\mathcal Z} p(\mathbf x, \mathcal D)Z=p(D)=∫Z​p(x,D)

The Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence

To formulate inference as an optimization problem, we need to choose an approximating family QQQ and an optimization objective J(q)J(q)J(q), for which the KL divergence is a candidate. The KL divergence between two distributions qqq and ppp with discrete support is defined as

KL(q∥p)=∑xq(x)log⁡q(x)p(x)=Eq[log⁡q(x)p(x)]\text{KL}(q\|p)=\sum_{\mathbf x} q(x)\log\frac{q(\mathbf x)}{p(\mathbf x)}=\mathbb{E}_q\left[\log\frac{q(\mathbf x)}{p(\mathbf x)}\right]KL(q∥p)=x∑​q(x)logp(x)q(x)​=Eq​[logp(x)q(x)​]

Properties:

  • KL(q∥p)≥0 ∀q,pKL(q\|p)\geq 0 \space \forall q, pKL(q∥p)≥0 ∀q,p.
  • KL(q∥p)=0  ⟺  q=pKL(q\|p)=0\iff q=pKL(q∥p)=0⟺q=p
  • KL(q∥p)≠KL(p∥q)KL(q\|p)\neq KL(p\|q)KL(q∥p)=KL(p∥q) (i.e. the KL divergence is not symmetric, hence not a distance)

The variational lower bound

Assume that ppp can be evaluated up to a normalization constant. Optimizing KL(q∥p)KL(q\|p)KL(q∥p) directly is not possible because of the potentially intractable normalization constant ZZZ. Instead, we'll work with the unnormalized probability p~\tilde pp~​

J(q)≜KL(q∥p~)=∑Zq(x)log⁡q(x)p~(x)J(q)\triangleq\text{KL}(q\|\tilde{p})=\sum_{\mathcal Z} q(\mathbf x)\log\frac{q(\mathbf x)}{\tilde{p}(\mathbf x)}J(q)≜KL(q∥p~​)=Z∑​q(x)logp~​(x)q(x)​

Not only is this function tractable, but it also has the following important property

J(q)=∑Zq(x)log⁡q(x)p~(x)=∑Zq(x)log⁡q(x)p(x)−log⁡Z=KL(q∥p)−log⁡Z\begin{align*} J(q) &= \sum_{\mathcal Z} q(\mathbf x)\log\frac{q(\mathbf x)}{\tilde{p}(\mathbf x)} \\ &= \sum_{\mathcal Z} q(\mathbf x)\log\frac{q(\mathbf x)}{p(\mathbf x)}-\log Z \\ &= \text{KL}(q\|p)-\log Z \end{align*}J(q)​=Z∑​q(x)logp~​(x)q(x)​=Z∑​q(x)logp(x)q(x)​−logZ=KL(q∥p)−logZ​

Since ZZZ is a constant, by minimizing J(q)J(q)J(q), we will force qqq to become close to ppp. Additionally, since KL divergence is always non-negative, we see that J(q)J(q)J(q) is an upper bound on the negative log-likelihood (NLL):

J(q)=KL(q∥p)−log⁡Z≥−log⁡Z=−log⁡p(D)J(q)= \text{KL}(q\|p)-\log Z\geq -\log Z= -\log p(\mathcal D)J(q)=KL(q∥p)−logZ≥−logZ=−logp(D)

Alternativey, we can maximize the energy functional L(q)\mathcal L(q)L(q), which is a lower bound of the log-likelihood of the data

L(q)≜−J(q)=−KL(q∥p)+log⁡Z≤log⁡Z=log⁡p(D)\mathcal L(q)\triangleq-J(q)=-\text{KL}(q\|p)+\log Z\leq \log Z = \log p(\mathcal D)L(q)≜−J(q)=−KL(q∥p)+logZ≤logZ=logp(D)

Therefore, L(q)\mathcal L(q)L(q) is also called the evidence lower bound (ELBO) or the variational lower bound.

Alternative interpretations of the variational objective

In statistical physics, J(q)J(q)J(q) is called the variational free energy or the Hellmholtz free energy, and is equal to the expected energy minus the entropy of the system

J(q)=Eq[log⁡q(x)]+Eq[−log⁡p~(x)]=−H(q)+Eq[E(x)]J(q)=\mathbb E_q[\log q(x)] + \mathbb E_q[-\log\tilde p(x)]=-\mathbb H(q)+\mathbb E_q[E(x)]J(q)=Eq​[logq(x)]+Eq​[−logp~​(x)]=−H(q)+Eq​[E(x)]

Another formuation of J(q)J(q)J(q) as the expected NLL plus a penalty term that measures how far the approximate posterior is from the exact prior

J(q)=Eq[log⁡q(x)−log⁡p(x)p(D∣x)]=Eq[log⁡q(x)−log⁡p(x)−log⁡p(D∣x)]=Eq[−log⁡p(D∣x)]+KL(q∥p)\begin{align*} J(q) &=\mathbb E_q[\log q(\mathbf x)-\log p(\mathbf x)p(\mathcal D|\mathbf x)] \\ &=\mathbb E_q[\log q(\mathbf x)-\log p(\mathbf x) - \log p(\mathcal D|\mathbf x)] \\ &=\mathbb E_q[-\log p(\mathcal D|\mathbf x)]+\text{KL}(q\|p) \end{align*}J(q)​=Eq​[logq(x)−logp(x)p(D∣x)]=Eq​[logq(x)−logp(x)−logp(D∣x)]=Eq​[−logp(D∣x)]+KL(q∥p)​

Forwards or reverse KL?

KL(q∥p)≠KL(p∥q)\text{KL}(q\|p)\neq\text{KL}(p\|q)KL(q∥p)=KL(p∥q) and both divergences equal zero when q=pq=pq=p, but assign different penalties when q≠pq\neq pq=p.

Computational-wise, optimizing KL(q∥p)\text{KL}(q\|p)KL(q∥p) involves an expectation w.r.t. qqq, while KL(p∥q)\text{KL}(p\|q)KL(p∥q) requires computing expecatations w.r.t ppp, which is typically intractable to even evaluate. The choice of divergence also affects the returned solution when the approximating family Q\mathcal QQ does not contain the true ppp.

First, consider the reverse KL, KL(q∥p)\text{KL}(q\|p)KL(q∥p), aka an I-projection or information projection,

KL(q∥p)=∑xq(x)log⁡q(x)p(x)\text{KL}(q\|p)=\sum_xq(\mathbf x)\log\frac{q(\mathbf x)}{p(\mathbf x)}KL(q∥p)=∑x​q(x)logp(x)q(x)​

This is is infinite if p(x)=0p(x)=0p(x)=0 and q(x)>0q(x)>0q(x)>0. Thus if p(x)=0p(\mathbf x)=0p(x)=0 we must ensure q(x)=0q(\mathbf x)=0q(x)=0. We say that KL(q∥p)\text{KL}(q\|p)KL(q∥p) is zero-forcing for qqq and it will typically under-estimate the support of ppp.

Now consider the forwards KL, KL(p∥q)\text{KL}(p\|q)KL(p∥q), aka an M-projection or momemt project,

KL(p∥q)=∑xp(x)log⁡p(x)q(x)\text{KL}(p\|q)=\sum_xp(\mathbf x)\log\frac{p(\mathbf x)}{q(\mathbf x)}KL(p∥q)=∑x​p(x)logq(x)p(x)​

This is infinite if q(x)=0q(\mathbf x)=0q(x)=0 and p(x)>0p(\mathbf x)>0p(x)>0. Thus, if p(x)>0p(\mathbf x)>0p(x)>0 we must ensure q(x)>0q(\mathbf x)>0q(x)>0. We say that KL(p∥q)\text{KL}(p\|q)KL(p∥q) is zero-avoiding for qqq and it will typically over-estimate the support of ppp.

The difference between these methods is illustrated in the figure below, where ppp is the blue contours and qqq red. We see that when the true distribution is multimodal, using the forwards KL is a bad idea (assuming qqq is unimodal), since the resulting poster mode/mean will be in a region of low density, right between the two peaks.

The mean-field method

The next step in our development of variational inference concerns the choice of approximating family Q\mathcal QQ. One of the most popular forms is called the mean-field approximation. In this approach, we assume the posterior is a fully factorized approximation of the form

q(x)=∏iqi(xi)q(\mathbf x)=\prod_i q_i(\mathbf x_i)q(x)=∏i​qi​(xi​)

Our goal is to solve this optimization problem:

min⁡q1,...,qnJ(q)≡max⁡q1,...,qnL(q)\min_{q_1, ..., q_n} J(q)\equiv \max_{q_1, ..., q_n} \mathcal L(q)q1​,...,qn​min​J(q)≡q1​,...,qn​max​L(q)

where we optimize over the parameters of each marginal distribution qiq_iqi​.

The standard way of performing this optimization problem is via coordinate descent over the qjq_jqj​. We iterate over j=1,2,...,nj=1, 2, ..., nj=1,2,...,n and for each jjj we optimize KL(q∥p)\text{KL}(q\|p)KL(q∥p) over qjq_jqj​ while keeping the other "coordinates" q−j=∏i≠jqiq_{-j}=\prod_{i\neq j}q_iq−j​=∏i=j​qi​ fixed. This has a simple closed form solution:

log⁡qj(xj)←Eq−j[log⁡p~(x)]+const\log q_j(x_j)\leftarrow\mathbb E_{q_{-j}}[\log\tilde p(x)]+\text{const}logqj​(xj​)←Eq−j​​[logp~​(x)]+const

Notice that:

  • Both sides of the above equation contain univariate functions of xj\mathbf x_jxj​: we are thus replacing q(xj)q(x_j)q(xj​) with another function of the same form. The constant term is a normalization constant for the new distribution.
  • On the right-hand side, we are taking an expectation of a sum of factors log⁡p~(x)=∑klog⁡ϕ(xk)\log\tilde p(\mathbf x)=\sum_k\log\phi(\mathbf x_k)logp~​(x)=∑k​logϕ(xk​). Only factors belonging to the Markov blanket of xj\mathbf x_jxj​ are a function of xjx_jxj​; the rest are constant w.r.t xj\mathbf x_jxj​ and can be pushed to the constant term. Sicne we are replacing the neigghbouring values by their mean value, the method is known is mean field.
  • Tis leaves us with an expectation over a much smaller number of factors.

The result of this is a procedure that iteratively fits a fully-factored q(x)=∏ipi(xi)q(\mathbf x)=\prod_ip_i(\mathbf x_i)q(x)=∏i​pi​(xi​) that approximates ppp in terms of KL(q∥p)\text{KL}(q\|p)KL(q∥p). After each step of coordinate descent, we increase the variational lower bound, tightening it around log⁡Z\log ZlogZ. In the end, the factors qj(xj)q_j(\mathbf x_j)qj​(xj​) will not quite equal the true marginal distributions p(xj)p(\mathbf x_j)p(xj​), but they will often be good enough for many practical purposes, such as determining max⁡xjp(xj)\max_{\mathbf x_j} p(\mathbf x_j)maxxj​​p(xj​).

Loopy belief propagation (LBP)

LBP is a very simple approximate inference algorithm for discrete (or Gaussian) graphical models. The basic idea: we apply the belief propagation algorithm to the graph, even if it has loops (i.e., even if it is not a tree).

The algo below outlines LBP for pairwise models. To handle models with higher-order clique potentials (which include directed models where some nodes have more than one parent), we simply apply LBP on the factor graph.

Note that convergence is not guaranteed.

LBP for a pairwise MRF

  1. Input: node potentials ψs(xs)\psi_s(x_s)ψs​(xs​), edge potentials ψst(xs,xt)\psi_{st}(x_s, x_t)ψst​(xs​,xt​).
  2. Initialize messages ms→t(xt)=1m_{s\rightarrow t(x_t)}=1ms→t(xt​)​=1 for all edges s−ts-ts−t.
  3. Initialize beliefs bels(xs)=1\text{bel}_s(x_s)=1bels​(xs​)=1 for all nodes sss.
  4. Repeat until beliefs don't change significantly:
    • Send message on each edge
    • ms→t(xt)=∑xs(ψs(xs)ψst(xs,xt)∏u∈N(s)∖tmu→s(xs))m_{s\rightarrow t}(x_t)=\sum_{x_s}\left(\psi_s(x_s)\psi_{st}(x_s, x_t)\prod_{u\in N(s)\setminus t}m_{u\rightarrow s}(x_s)\right)ms→t​(xt​)=∑xs​​(ψs​(xs​)ψst​(xs​,xt​)∏u∈N(s)∖t​mu→s​(xs​)).

Convergence of LBP

The computation tree visualizes the messages that are passed as the algorithm proceeds. The key insight is that TTT iterations of LBP is equivalent to exact computation in a computation tree of height T+1T+1T+1. If the strengths of the connections on the edges is sufficiently weak, then the influence of the leaves on the root will diminish over time, and convergence will occur.

Making LBP converge

One simple way to reduce the chacne of oscillation is to use damping i.e. insteand of sending the messages MtskM_{ts}^kMtsk​, we send a damped messaged of the form

M~tsk(xs)=λMts(xs)+(1−λ)M~tsk−1(xs)\tilde M_{ts}^k(x_s)=\lambda M_{ts}(x_s)+(1-\lambda)\tilde M_{ts}^{k-1}(x_s)M~tsk​(xs​)=λMts​(xs​)+(1−λ)M~tsk−1​(xs​)

Increasing the convergence rate: message scheduling

The standard approach when implementing LBP is to perform synchronyous updates, where all nodes absorb messages in parallel, and then send out messages in parallel i.e. the new messages at iteration k+1k+1k+1 are computed in parallel using

mk+1=(f1(mk),...,fE(mk))\mathbf m^{k+1}=(f_1(\mathbf m^k), ..., f_E(\mathbf m^k))mk+1=(f1​(mk),...,fE​(mk))

where EEE is the number of edges and fst(m)f_{st}(\mathbf m)fst​(m) is the function that computes the message for edge s→ts\rightarrow ts→t given all the old messages. This is analogous to the Jacobi method for solving linear systems of equations.

It is well known that the Gauss-Seidel method, which performs asynchronous updates in a fixed round-robin fashion, converges faster when solving linear systems of equations. We can apply the same idea to LBP, using updates of the form

mkk+1=fi({mjk+1∣j<i},{mjk∣j>i})\mathbf m_k^{k+1}=f_i(\{\mathbf m_j^{k+1}|j<i\}, \{\mathbf m_j^k|j>i\})mkk+1​=fi​({mjk+1​∣j<i},{mjk​∣j>i})

where the message for edge iii is computed using new messages (iteration k+1k+1k+1) from edges earlier in the ordering, and using old messages (iteration kkk) from edges later in the ordering.
